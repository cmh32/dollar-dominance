The Future of Dollar Dominance

An Analysis of Emerging Threats to Dollar Hegemony

Fritz Albers, Monica Gronseth, Lizzie Heywood, Colin Hughes, Duyen Pham  ·  April 2026

Watch: Video Report

The dollar is the world's premier currency, but it is under threat. We have identified three forces that could affect its dominance over the coming decade and beyond, including the rise of digital currencies, the growing international role of China's yuan, and declining global trust US economic policies and institutions. To be sure, the dollar is still dominant and remains the world's most important currency. However, these forces will challenge its preeminence in the years to come, and the US should begin considering policy adjustments that prevent its decline.

Digital Currencies Could Act as a Digital Disruptor

$33T
in stablecoin transaction volume in 2025

The rapid expansion of digital currencies, including cryptocurrencies, stablecoins, and central bank digital currencies (CBDCs), poses a systemic challenge to dollar hegemony. Alternative payment rails are continuing to scale, with stablecoin transaction volumes reaching approximately $33 trillion in 2025. CBDCs like China's digital yuan aim to bypass traditional dollar-based systems like SWIFT, enabling countries to circumvent the pipes of U.S. financial infrastructure. While these new systems are unlikely to completely displace the USD's usefulness in the near term, these innovations weaken its network effects and long-term monopoly characteristics and offer alternatives for countries facing U.S. sanctions.

The Chinese Yuan Is Gaining Prominence

8%
of global trade finance now settled in yuan

The Chinese yuan is gaining traction in global trade and finance, particularly within China-centric economic ecosystems. It now accounts for over 50% of China's cross-border payments and approximately 8% of global trade finance, up significantly from prior years. Its growing role in FX markets and bilateral trade settlement reflects a broader trend of incremental "de-dollarization." Although the dollar remains dominant, the yuan's steady adoption, especially across emerging markets, signals a shift toward a more multipolar currency system.

Trust in the US Dollar Is Eroding

156%
projected US debt-to-GDP in coming decades

US policy volatility, weakening institutions, and ballooning debt are undermining global trust in the dollar. Trade barriers rise and fall without warning, US institutions like the Fed face attacks, and public confidence is weakening in Congress as a political check. Simultaneously, US debt continues to balloon. The Congressional Budget Office projects debt to reach 156 percent of US GDP within the next few decades, and Moody's recently downgraded US credit. To be sure, the dollar remains strong. 57 percent of the world's foreign currency reserves are dollar denominated, and 88 percent of all trades involve the dollar. However, diminishing trust in the US could lead to its gradual erosion, even if it does not collapse in the near term.

Policy Recommendations to Maintain Dollar Dominance

To sustain dollar leadership, we advocate a forward-looking strategy centered on usability, trust, and technological adaptation. This includes modernizing digital infrastructure by regulating and integrating dollar-backed stablecoins (e.g., full reserves, audits, convertibility) and enhancing payment systems through tokenized settlement infrastructure for cross-border finance. Fixing the plumbing will help ensure the dollar remains embedded in next-generation financial rails. In addition, the US should restore fiscal credibility by addressing long-term debt dynamics and reinforcing institutional stability. Fiscal strength, along with a principled rule of law and politically insulated institutions, underpins currency trust.
